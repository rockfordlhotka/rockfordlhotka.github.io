There is a lot of fear-mongering and hand-wringing about AI right now, and what it will do to jobs and the economy.

I think some of those concerns are _extremely_ valid, and I really am very concerned that our politictians, business leaders, and social leaders aren't ready to help us all navigate the coming changes. I suspect they are at a level similar to the Industrial Revolution, or how we ended up with the Rust Belt in the US.

My view is that a lot of the fear-mongering is overblown as well. While I strongly suspect we're headed for a period of pain as a lot of jobs change radically, I'm optimistic that the long-term result has the potential to be positive.

The thing is, I'm not an economist, and I'm going to try and stay in my lane: the software development industry. In this case, our industry is a canary in the coal mine, and I've started to develop some thoughts on what AI means to us as software developers, and what it means to the software development industry.

> It is important to realize that the "software development industry" is a bit odd, because most of the people in this industry are technically getting paid by _other industries_. Most businesses have IT departments, and there are a lot of software developers working in those departments. You could say they are in the healthcare, or manufacturing, or some other industry; but really they are part of the software development industry, because they are writing software. So when I talk about the software development industry, I'm talking about all the people who write software, regardless of what industry they are in.

In my view, AI is almost certainly going to disrupt any tasks that involve sitting at a computer screen or dealing with information. Ultimately, this is not just software development, but accounting, marketing, legal work, and a lot of other jobs. But software development is a canary in the coal mine, because we are the ones who are building the tools that will be used to disrupt those other industries.

I'm already at the point where I don't manually write any code anymore. When Opus 4.5 came out in November 2025 it changed the world, and between the models and tooling available from Anthropic and Microsoft, I have learned how to cocreate software with AI.

Not just creating software, but deployment, configuring and managing infrastructure, the devops pipelines, unit testing, some user acceptance testing, documentation, and a lot of other tasks that are part of software development. I can do all of those things with AI, and I can do them faster and better than I could before.

When I say faster and better, I mean easily 30 times faster, and with the ability to simultaneously create the code, documentation, deployment scripts, tests, and other critical assets. In the "old world", this would have been far more of a serial process: create and test the code, figure out deployment, get user testing feedback, write the documentation, etc. Now it is all happening in parallel, and the AI is helping me to keep track of all of it, and to make sure that everything is consistent and working together.

So if a software developer or software engineer no longer writes code, or sets up infrastructure, or all the other things on which we used to be measured, what does that mean for the software development industry?

To answer this question, we need to do some introspection. What is the _real value_ that a software developer/engineer/architect provides? 

Was it that we can type fast? No. Was it that we learned esoteric programming languages and frameworks? No. Was it that we could write code that was efficient and performant? No.

We are _systems thinkers_. At least "good" developers are systems thinkers.

What does this mean? It means that we understand how to take a problem, break it down into smaller pieces, and then figure out how to solve those pieces in a way that fits together to solve the larger problem. We understand how to design systems that are maintainable, scalable, and resilient. We understand how to think about the user experience, and how to design software that is easy to use and meets the needs of the users.

We understand how to think about the trade-offs between different design decisions, and how to make those trade-offs in a way that balances the needs of the users, the business, and the technical constraints.

We understand how to think about the long-term implications of our design decisions, and how to design software that is maintainable and extensible over time.

Hopefully, we understand how to think about the ethical implications of our design decisions, and how to design software that is responsible and ethical. To be inclusive, to be accessible, to be secure, to be private, and to be transparent.

My observation, after almost 40 years in the software development industry, and working across many different vertical industries, is that most humans are _not systems thinkers_. Sadly, this includes quite a few "coders" and "programmers" and other labels people hold within the software development industry.

It is also the case that people _outside_ software development may be systems thinkers. My neighbor is a carpenter and contractor, and I'd certainly say he is a systems thinker. He understands how to design and build a house that is maintainable, scalable, and resilient. He understands how to think about the user experience, and how to design a house that is easy to use and meets the needs of the users. He understands how to think about the trade-offs between different design decisions, and how to make those trade-offs in a way that balances the needs of the users, the business, and the technical constraints. He understands how to think about the long-term implications of his design decisions, and how to design a house that is maintainable and extensible over time.

This systems thinking skill is not unique to software development, but it is a critical skill for software developers. It is the skill that allows us to create software that is maintainable, scalable, and resilient. It is the skill that allows us to create software that meets the needs of the users, the business, and the technical constraints.

I argue that this is one of the key skills that, in the forseeable future, will not be easily replaced by AI.

In fact, I'm using these skills more than ever, because the _rest of the work_ is highly compressed by AI. The cycle time between idea and implementation is now so short that I have to be able to think about the system as a whole, and how all the pieces fit together, in order to keep up with the pace of development. Every day, all day.

In the radio play [Ruby 3](https://www.zbs.org/shop/product/ruby-3-mp3/?utm_source=copilot.com) there are scenes where characters are creating constructs in their minds. I've always found that to be a very powerful metaphor for what we do as software developers. We are creating constructs in our minds, and then we are using those constructs to create software that solves problems.

Writing code was the clunky way we had to translate these beautiful constructs in our minds into something that a computer could understand. Now, with AI, we can directly translate those constructs into software, without having to go through the clunky process of writing code.

At least that's where I think we are headed - rapidly.

So while I do think that AI will entirely disrupt the software development industry, I think it will actually _unleash_ the creativity and productivity of folks who are systems thinkers: who can take business problems, visualize the system and solution in their minds, and can work with AI to cause those mental constructs to become software that has a tangible effect in the real world.

I think the software development industry will be transformed into a much more creative and collaborative industry, where the focus is on designing systems that solve problems, rather than on writing code. I think we will see a lot of new tools and platforms that will make it easier for people to create software, and I think we will see a lot of new opportunities for people who are systems thinkers to create software that has a real impact on the world.
